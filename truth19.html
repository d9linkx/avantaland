Actionable Checklist

The catastrophe happened at 2 AM on a Tuesday. Our payment system went down during a critical growth experiment. Revenue stopped flowing. Panic set in.

The problem? The engineer who built the payment integration had left the company three months earlier. The only documentation was a single README file that said: "Uses Stripe API. See code."

I watched our remaining engineers spend eight frantic hours reverse-engineering what should have taken twenty minutes to fix. They had to decipher variable names and guess at original design decisions.

By the time they restored service, we'd lost: Thousands of dollars in abandoned transactions. A full week‚Äôs worth of engineering morale. The original development time (it took longer to fix than it did to build).

That‚Äôs when I realized that documentation is compound interest. I learned this lesson again when a new customer success hire spent her first week asking the same five questions. The entire engineering team lost hours answering questions that should have been in a single document.

Documentation feels like bureaucracy. The kind of thing big, slow companies do. But I've come to see it differently: documentation is compound interest for your time. Every hour you invest in it saves you ten hours later.

The brutal truth about business knowledge

Documentation isn't about polished manuals. It's about capturing knowledge that would otherwise be trapped in individual brains.

Why businesses especially need documentation:

The bus factor: How many people would need to get hit by a bus before your project becomes unrecoverable? In many businesses, this number is one.

Onboarding acceleration: Every new hire takes 3x longer to become productive without it.

Context preservation: Code tells you what; it rarely tells you why. Documentation prevents you from re-debating settled questions.
