Truth #11

Building something for yourself is tempting but dangerous

Executive Summary

I built my first startup for the person I wished I was: a digital nomad working from beaches in Lagos, needing elegant productivity tools to manage my "empire." The product was beautiful: a minimalist task manager with Pomodoro timers and elegant analytics.

Actionable Checklist

I built my first startup for the person I wished I was: a digital nomad working from beaches in Lagos, needing elegant productivity tools to manage my "empire." The product was beautiful: a minimalist task manager with Pomodoro timers and elegant analytics.

I was my own perfect customer. I used it every day. I assumed everyone would.

The problem was, I wasn't building for the person I actually was: a stressed founder working 80-hour weeks, juggling context switches, and needing to just not forget the damn payroll.

My beautiful app was useless for that reality. When a real crisis hit, I'd abandon it and go back to a chaotic but functional combination of Google Docs, Calendar alerts, and Scribbled notes.

The wake-up call came when I got my first 100 beta users. They didn't want beauty; they wanted Salesforce integrations and team task assignments. I found myself getting angry at them. "They don't get it," I'd mutter.

What makes you a "weirdo" in business terms?

You have "Founder-Product Fit" when your solution works for exactly one person: you. This happens when your needs are:

Too niche: You're a power user in a sea of casual users.

Too privileged: You have technical skills or resources your users lack.

Too idiosyncratic: Your workflow is unique to your own strange habits.

Not painful enough: It‚Äôs an annoyance you enjoy fixing, not a crisis others will pay for.

The three traps of building for yourself

The "everyone is like me" fallacy: Assuming your high tolerance for complexity and specific priorities are universal.

The "solution blindness" trap: Becoming so enamored with your elegant solution that you stop questioning if the problem is worth solving for anyone else.

The "feedback filter" problem: Dismissing negative feedback as a lack of "good taste" on the user's part.

Summary

Your own frustration is an excellent starting pistol, but a terrible finish line. Use your personal pain as a source of empathy and insight, not as a product roadmap.
