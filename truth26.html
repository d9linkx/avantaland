Truth #26

Marketing starts before product; awareness is traction

Actionable Checklist

Imagine two chefs opening a restaurant. Chef A finds a location, spends six months designing a beautiful interior, and perfects a menu in total secrecy. On opening day, he flips the sign to "Open" and waits. The street is empty. He's confused and heartbroken. He built a perfect restaurant, so why aren't customers lining up?

Chef B, nine months before her doors open, starts a food blog. She documents her journey finding local farmers, shares stories about recipes, and builds an email list of 3,000 enthusiasts. She hosts pop-up dinners to test her dishes and lets future customers vote on menu items.

The result of building in public: When Chef B's restaurant finally opens, it's not an opening. It's a victory lap. Her email list ensures a packed house for weeks. Her blog readers become her first evangelists. The local press is already eager to cover the story.

The lesson: Marketing isn't the megaphone you use on launch day; it's the foundation you build long before you lay the first brick.

The "marketing-first" philosophy

The "if you build it, they will come" philosophy is responsible for more business failures than almost any other miscalculation. Pre-launch marketing is the systematic process of de-risking your business by building an audience and validating demand before you commit significant resources.

Summary

Launch day should not be the starting pistol; it should be the finish line of your first marathon. The real work begins with the first conversation about the problem and the first email address on your list.

Stop building in the dark. Turn the lights on, invite people in, and let them watch (and help) you build.
