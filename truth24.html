Truth #24

Early adopters are gold; casual users are noise

Executive Summary

I watched two founders take opposite paths. One chased vanity metrics, acquiring thousands of casual users who quickly churned. The other made her product harder to access, filtering for a small group of deeply committed early adopters. While the first founder failed, the second built a product 50 users loved and paid for. Early adopters are not just your first customers; they are your co-creators. They tolerate imperfection because they feel the pain acutely. They provide the signal in the noise. Stop chasing the crowd and start cultivating the few who will become your fiercest advocates.

Actionable Checklist

I watched two founders in the same co-working space take completely different approaches to their first users.

Founder A was obsessed with vanity metrics: total signups, download numbers, and social media followers. He spent heavily on Facebook ads targeting broad audiences. His user count grew rapidly, but his product went nowhere.

Founder B did something that seemed counterintuitive: she actively made her product harder to access. She set up a short application form and conducted 15-minute onboarding calls with every prospective user. Her growth was painfully slow at first: just 3-5 new users per week.

But these users were different: They came with specific problems. They sent detailed bug reports. They suggested features with exact use cases. They passionately explained what they needed and why.

Within three months, Founder B had built something remarkable: a product that 80% of her small user base used daily and described as "indispensable." Founder A had thousands of "users" but no real product-market fit. Founder B had 50 fiercely loyal early adopters and was generating revenue.

The lesson: In the early days, early adopters are not just your first users. They should be your co-creators, evangelists, and your most valuable source of market truth.

The psychology of the early adopter

Early adopters are the 1% of users who see the potential in your incomplete, rough-around-the-edges solution because they feel the pain point acutely enough to tolerate imperfection.

Trying to appeal to everyone too early is like trying to host a black-tie event in a construction site. People will show up, but they won't stay, and they'll tell others not to bother.

Summary

Early adopters are the foundation upon which great products are built. They provide the signal in the noise and the momentum in the early struggle.

Stop chasing casual users who will never love you and start cultivating the small group of people who will become your most passionate advocates. Win the few before you try to win the many.
