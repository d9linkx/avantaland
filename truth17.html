Features are distractions. Focus on outcomes

Actionable Checklist

I sat in a product meeting that perfectly captured the feature obsession. Our team was debating whether to build a dark mode for our SaaS platform. The arguments were passionate: "All the best apps have it!" "It's table stakes now!"

We were about to greenlight two weeks of development time when our newest engineer, Maya, asked a simple question: "What user outcome does dark mode actually drive?"

The room went silent. We had feature arguments, but no outcome arguments. We spent the next hour digging into the data and realized something: The users "begging" for dark mode represented 0.3% of our user base. Our key metrics‚Äîretention, time in app, conversion‚Äîwould be completely unaffected.

We were about to burn precious engineering cycles on a feature that would make no measurable difference to our business. I've watched businesses die this way.

One team built 27 different integrations but couldn't get users to complete basic onboarding. Another had a "comprehensive" analytics dashboard that 2% of users ever looked at, while cancellations climbed.

The most successful product decision I ever made was saying "no" to 90% of feature requests. We maintained a brutally simple product that did one thing exceptionally well.

The lesson: Our competitors had more checkboxes on their feature comparison charts, but we had better retention and higher conversion. They were building features; we were delivering outcomes.

Outputs vs. Outcomes

The fundamental confusion lies in mistaking what you build for the value it creates:

Outputs: What you build (features, code, designs).

Outcomes: The measurable changes in user behavior that drive business value.

Why features become toxic distractions

The "checkbox mentality": Building features just because competitors have them, leading to a bloated product that does many things poorly.

The "solutioneering" trap: Teams love solving complex technical problems. Intellectual curiosity often drives the roadmap instead of actual user value.

The "vanity metric" deception: It is easier to say "we shipped 15 features" than "we improved retention by 20%." Features create an illusion of progress.

The forever cost: Every feature you add isn't just a one-time cost. It must be maintained, documented, and supported forever. Features are like children; conceiving them is the easy part.

Summary

Your product's value isn't measured in features; it's measured in the impact you deliver. Stop counting your features and start measuring your results. The most sophisticated feature in the world is worthless if it doesn't change user behavior in ways that matter.

Build less, but build what matters. Your product (and your users) will thank you for the restraint.
