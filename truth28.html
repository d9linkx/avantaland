Truth #28

You can't "growth hack" a product that nobody wants

Executive Summary

Actionable Checklist

I saw a startup burn through $500,000 in venture funding on the most brilliant growth hack I've ever seen. And it destroyed them.

They'd built a meal-planning app that was... fine. Not great, not terrible. But they discovered a loophole in Facebook's ad platform that let them acquire users for just $0.10 each when everyone else was paying $5.

They went all in. They scaled their ad spend to $50,000 a month. Their user graph shot up and to the right. They raised more money based on these "traction" metrics. Investors were thrilled. The growth was undeniable.

There was just one problem: their product wasn't very good. The meal plans were generic. The shopping list feature was buggy. The user experience was confusing. But buried under the avalanche of new users, these problems seemed minor.

"We'll fix the product later," the founder told me. "Right now, we're focused on growth."

Then Facebook closed the loophole. Their CAC jumped from $0.10 to $8.00 overnight. And that's when the truth revealed itself: their users were leaving as fast as they were coming. Their retention curve looked like a cliff.

Without the growth hack propping them up, the fundamental weakness of their product was exposed. They had massive user acquisition but zero product-market fit. Six months later, they were out of business. The growth hack hadn't saved them‚Äîit had just given them a more expensive way to fail.

The seduction of growth hacks is that they offer the illusion of progress without the hard work of building something people actually want. This is like putting rocket fuel in a car with a broken engine. You'll move very fast until you explode.

The fundamental relationship between growth and PMF

Product-market fit is about building something people want so badly they'll use it, pay for it, and tell others about it‚Äîeven if your marketing is terrible. Growth hacks are about removing friction between people who want your product and actually using it.

Summary

Growth hacks are amplifiers, not creators of value. They can make a good product successful faster, but they can't make a bad product successful.

Focus on making your product so good that your biggest growth problem is keeping up with organic demand. Then, and only then, use growth hacks to scale what's already working. Don't use growth as a substitute for value. Use it as an accelerator of value. Build something people want first; hack your way to more of them second.
