Truth #22

A "good" product shipped today beats a "perfect" one that never exists

Executive Summary

I watched a founder strangle her business with perfectionism. She spent eight months refining her "perfect" kitchen gadget, always finding one more thing to fix before launch. Meanwhile, a competitor launched an "embarrassingly basic" version. They got to market, learned from customers, and iterated. By the time her perfect product finally launched, nobody cared. The market had moved on. Her company died of perfection. A "good" product shipped today is infinitely better than a "perfect" one that never exists. Perfectionism is just fear in disguise. Ship, learn, and improve.

Actionable Checklist

I watched a founder slowly strangle her business with perfection. For eight months, she'd been refining "the perfect product", in this case, an innovative kitchen gadget.

She had prototypes, manufacturing partners, and impressive funding. But she refused to launch. Every time they approached a production date, she'd find something else that needed perfecting: The handle needed better ergonomics. The packaging needed premium finishes. The manufacturing needed one more quality check.

I'd ask her every month: "When are you launching?" Her answer was always some variation of "We're almost ready; just two more weeks." Those two weeks stretched into eight months of perfecting a product nobody was using.

The breaking point came when a competitor launched what she called an "embarrassingly basic" version of the same concept. It was simpler, used cheaper materials, and had minimal packaging. But it was on shelves.

Six months later, that "embarrassing" competitor had: 50,000 units sold, A clear product roadmap based on real customer feedback, and Rapid expansion into new retailers.

My friend finally launched her perfect product. It was beautifully designed, impeccably manufactured, and comprehensive in its features. And nobody cared.

The market had moved on. Customers were already committed to the "good enough" solution that had been improving alongside their needs. Her perfect product solved yesterday's problems, while the competitor's imperfect product was solving today's.

She shut down the company three months later. The epitaph: "Died of perfection."

Perfectionism is procrastination in disguise

Perfectionism is the most seductive form of procrastination. It dresses up as quality control but is actually fear in disguise. Fear of judgment, fear of failure, fear of being seen as imperfect.

Summary

Perfection is the enemy of progress. Every day you delay shipping is a day you delay learning. The market doesn't reward perfect products/services. It rewards solutions that evolve to meet market needs.

Your first version is just the beginning of a conversation with your customers, not the final word. So, ship fast, learn fast, and iterate relentlessly. The lessons you learn from shipping will always be more valuable than the features you perfect in isolation.
